Abstract 
The suppression of transposable elements in the germline is critical for saf eguarding f ertility. Here we identified that DAXX (death-associated 
protein 6), a potent transcription repressor and an H3.3 chaperone, was indispensable for silencing of transposable elements during spermatoge- 
nesis. Daxx germline conditional knockout mice exhibited delayed meiotic progression, spermiogenesis defects, and an age-dependent decline 
in fertility. The ablation of DAXX led to activation of evolutionarily young long interspersed nuclear element-1 (LINE1) and endogenous retrovirus 
(ER V) subf amilies. Further mechanistic study re v ealed that DAXX participated in the repression of young LINE1s by regulating H3K9me3 en- 
richment and maintaining DNA methylation. In summary, this study identifies DAXX as a previously unknown regulator of spermatogenesis and 
demonstrates that it functions as an epigenetic regulator to silence young LINE1s.

Introduction 
DAXX (death-associated protein 6), which was initially iden- 
tified as an adaptor protein associated with the intracellular 
death domain of the apoptosis receptor Fas, is a highly con- 
served protein and plays diverse roles in fundamental bio- 
logical pathways such as Fas-triggered apoptosis, tumor de- 
velopment, antiviral defense, transcription, cell cycle regula- 
tion, proliferation, and multiple physiological functions [ 1 –3 ]. 
Biochemical and cell-based studies have demonstrated that 
DAXX serves as a specific chaperone for histone H3.3 that col- 
laborates with ATRX (alpha-thalassemia/mental retardation 
X-linked) to drive nucleosome assembly through replication- 
independent mechanisms. Furthermore, DAXX and ATRX fa- 
cilitate the deposition of H3.3 into repetitive genomic regions, 
including pericentromeric DNA, telomeres, and transposable 
elements (TEs) [ 3 , 4 ]. 
While retrotransposons function as evolutionary catalysts 
facilitating genomic diversification, they concurrently pose 
risks to host genome integrity. This is particularly true in germ 
cells when epigenetic control is relaxed, allowing for exten- 
sive genome-wide reprogramming. In mammals, the activa- 
tion of retrotransposons typically induces sterility [ 5 ]. To com- 
bat these risks, the mammalian germline deploys multilayered 
regulatory strategies to restrict retrotransposon activity dur- 
ing critical developmental windows, including transcriptional 
repression via DNA methylation and H3K9 methylation, and 
post-transcriptional silencing mediated by PIWI-interacting 
RNAs (piRNAs) [ 6 –8 ]. Long interspersed nuclear element-1 
(LINE1), the most abundant type of retrotransposon in the 
mammalian genome, poses a significant risk for infertility, es- 
pecially in mouse mutants that exhibit overactivated retro- 
transposons (LINE1 and/or IAP) in male germ cells [ 5 ]. Stud- 
ies have also demonstrated that RLTR10B sequences, such as 
those found in MMERVK10C — one of the youngest retro- 
transposons in the mouse genome — are robustly silenced dur- 
ing spermatogenesis by TRIM33, a ubiquitin ligase that has 
E3 ubiquitin ligase activity for A-MYB and regulates its abun- 
dance for the transcription of any retrotransposon [ 9 ]. 
DNA methylation constitutes the principal epigenetic mod- 
ification responsible for transcriptional repression of TEs [ 10 ]. 
DNA methyltransferase enzymes (DNMT3A, DNMT3B, and 
DNMT3C) and one cofactor, DNMT3L, mediate de novo 
DNA methylation, while DNMT1 maintains methylation pro- 
files during DNA replication [ 10 –17 ]. Although the mecha- 
nisms of DNA de novo methylation have been under scrutiny 
[ 18 –21 ], little is known about how the germ cells maintain 
DNA methylation to silence TEs persistently. The epigenetic 
reprogramming in primordial germ cells (PGCs) witnesses 
global methylation erasure, triggering transposon activation 
[ 10 ]. This developmental process engages piRNA biogenesis, 
where transposon-derived transcripts feed piRNA production 
[ 22 ]. These piRNAs subsequently guide the de novo methyla- 
tion pattern at TE promoters, initiating transcriptional repres- 
sion via a molecular mechanism that remains incompletely re- 
solved in mammals [ 14 , 23 –25 ]. However, how TE activity is 
persistently suppressed and its methylation is maintained dur- 
ing the long process of spermatogenesis remains poorly under- 
stood. As ongoing research continues to uncover novel factors 
involved in the epigenetic silencing of retrotransposons in the 
germ cells, understanding these mechanisms may provide vital 
insights into fertility preservation and maintenance of genome 
stability. 
Here, we generate a germ-cell-specific Daxx knockout 
mouse model by crossing Daxx flox/flox mice with Ddx4 -Cre 
transgenic mice as global DAXX deletion is embryonically 
lethal [ 26 ], and demonstrate that loss of Daxx in germ 
cells

results in delayed spermatogenesis, abnormal sperm 
morphology, and an age-dependent decline in male fertil- 
ity. Comprehensive transcriptome profiling reveals that loss 
of Daxx induces global gene dysregulation, resulting in up- 
regulation of apoptosis- and transcription-related genes. Me- 
chanically, DAXX mediates LINE1 silencing by maintain- 
ing the chromatin state, including H3K9me3 enrichment and 
DNA methylation.

Materials and

methods 
Mice 
Mice were maintained at the standard animal facility of 
Wuhan University according to the Regulations for the Ad- 
ministration of Affairs Concerning Experimental Animals of 
China. All animal experiments strictly adhered to the ethical 
standards and protocols approved by The Laboratory Animal 
Care and Use Committee of Wuhan University. Daxx flox/flox 
mice were generated by CRISPR/Cas9 [clustered regularly in- 
terspaced palindromic repeats (CRISPR)/CRISPR-associated 
protein 9] technology. Testis-specific Daxx depletion was 
achieved by crossing Daxx flox/flox mice with Ddx4 -Cre trans- 
genic mice (Jackson Laboratory 006 954) or Stra8 -GFPCre 
mice, which were gifted by Dr Ming-Han Tong from Shanghai 
Institute of Biochemistry and Cell Biology, Chinese Academy 
of Sciences, University of Chinese Academy of Sciences [ 27 ]. 
Genomic DNA isolated from mouse tails was used for geno- 
typing, and the primer sequences are listed in Supplementary 
Table S1 . 
Preparation of DAXX antibody 
For polyclonal antibody production, a recombinant mouse 
DAXX antigen was engineered by cloning its cDNA in-frame 
into the pET42b expression vector, generating N-terminal 
amino acids 626 –739 fused with a 6 × His tag. Follow- 
ing isopropyl- β- d -thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) induction 
in BL21-CodonPlus (DE3) Esc heric hia coli , the His-tagged 
DAXX protein was purified via nitrilotriacetic acid affinity 
chromatography. After extensive dialysis against phosphate- 
buffered saline (PBS), the antigenic preparation was utilized to 
immunize rabbits for antibody induction. The resulting anti- 
serum was then tested to assess immunological specificity, and 
was finally used for western blotting and immunofluorescence 
(IF) staining.

immunofluorescence staining, and TUNEL assay

lected immediately after euthanasia, fixed in Bouin’s solution 
for 24 h, and embedded in paraffin after dehydration. Sec- 
tions of 5 μm thickness were prepared using a microtome and 
mounted on slides. Following xylene-mediated deparaffiniza- 
tion, slides were stained with hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) 
for histological analysis. 
For chromosome spread and IF staining, spermatocyte nu- 
clear spreads were prepared as previously described [ 28 ]. Pri- 
mary antibodies used for IF staining were as follows: anti- 
DAXX (home made), anti-SYCP3 (home made), anti-SYCE1

Western blot 
Freshly collected organs/tissues from C57BL/6 mice were ho- 
mogenized by ultrasonic processing in ice-cold lysis buffer 
[62.5 mM Tris –HCl (pH 6.8), 3% sodium dodecylsulfate 
(SDS), 10% glycerol, and 20% β-mercaptoethanol] supple- 
mented with protease inhibitor cocktail. Then, the total pro- 
tein extracts were centrifuged at 20 000 g and 4 ◦C for 10 min. 
The supernatant supplemented with protein loading buffer 
was boiled at 95 ◦C for 10 –15 min and centrifuged at 20 000 
g and 4 ◦C for 10 min. Equal amounts of protein lysates were 
resolved by SDS –polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (PAGE) 
and transferred onto polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) mem- 
branes (0.45 μm pore size) by wet transferring in transfer 
buffer (25 mM Tris, 192 mM glycine, 0.02% SDS, 10% 
methanol, pH 8.3). Immunoreactive PVDF membranes were 
probed and developed with Super Signal™West Pico PLUS 
Chemiluminescent Substrate and G:Box Chemi-XRQ GENE 
Sys. Quantitative analysis of target proteins was performed 
by calibrating against β-ACTIN or glyceraldehyde phosphate 
dehydrogenase (GAPDH). 
Immunoprecipitation and mass spectrometry 
analysis 
Total protein extraction from wild-type (WT) mice testes 
was immunoprecipitated using DAXX antibody. Antibodies 
were first incubated with protein A/G magnetic beads at 4 ◦C 
for 4 h, followed by incubation with testes extracts at 4 ◦C 
overnight. Immunoprecipitated proteins were washed with IP 
buffer three times and then eluted with 0.2 M glycine (pH 2.0). 
Eluted proteins were then precipitated with four volumes of 
acetone, re-solubilized in urea, and digested with trypsin at 
37 ◦C for 20 h. Digested peptides were desalted using the C18 
Stage tips and analyzed by liquid chromatography –tandem 
mass spectrometry (LC –MS/MS) on an EASY-nLC system 
coupled to an Orbitrap Exploris 480 mass spectrometer 
(Thermo Fisher Scientific). 
Isolation of mouse germ cells 
Spermatocytes were isolated by using the fluorescence- 
activated cell sorting (FACS) method [ 29 , 30 ]. Briefly, the tu- 
nica albuginea of testes was removed in Dulbecco’s modified 
Eagle’s medium (DMEM), and the testes were digested with 
collagenase type I (500 μg ml −1 , 32 ◦C, 15 –20 min, orbital) 
then further digested with trypsin (200 μg ml −1 ) and DNase 
I (20 μg ml −1 ) at 32 ◦C by shaking at 100 rpm for 5 –10 min, 
and the digestion was terminated with 10% fetal bovine serum 
(FBS). Next, the cell suspension underwent filtration by pass- 
ing through a 70 μm filter followed by low-speed centrifuga- 
tion (at 500 g and 4 ◦C for 5 min). The cell pellet was finally 
resuspended in DMEM with 10% FBS, fluorescently labeled 
with Hoechst 33 342, and sorted via BD FACS Aria II [ 28 , 29 ]. 
Computer-assisted motility analysis 
One epididymis was collected from each adult mouse and 
placed in a 1.5 ml Eppendorf tube. The tissue was minced and 
incubated with 1 ml of pre-warmed 1 × PBS at 37 ◦C for 30 
min to release the sperm. Subsequently, sperm counts were de- 
termined using a hemocytometer. Quantitative kinematic pro- 
filing of spermatozoa was conducted using the motility mod- 
ule of a clinically validated multiparametric andrological as- 
sessment system (BEION platform, version 4.20).

Small RNA sequencing and analysis 
The adaptor sequences (A GATCGGAA GA GCA CA CGTCT) 
were trimmed from the 3 ′ ends of the raw fastq files using 
cutadapt (version 4.9) with parameters -m 16 –trimmed-only 
[ 31 ]. Small RNA reads ranging from 18 to 32 nt in length 
were retrieved from each sample, following the procedure es- 
tablished for piRNA analyses [ 19 ]. These reads were sub- 
sequently aligned to reference sequences including miRNA 
from miRBase, non-coding RNAs, piRNA clusters, and ge- 
nomic TE sequences retrieved from RepeatMasker (mm39) 
[ 35 ]. When mapping reads to retrotransposon elements, up 
to three mismatches were permitted. Reads corresponding to 
piRNAs (25 –32 nt) were categorized according to genomic 
annotations; those that did not align to any known genomic 
elements were classified as ‘other’. For individual piRNA ex- 
pression analysis, only mapped reads between 25 and 32 nt 
with counts exceeding 10 were retained and visualized in a 
scatter plot. For Ping-pong analysis of piRNAs, the consen- 
sus sequences of L1MdTf_I, L1MdGf_I, L1MdF_I, L1MdA_I, 
IAPEY, and IAPEZI were retrieved from Repbase [ 36 ]. Pro- 
cessed reads in FASTA format were aligned to these consensus 
sequences, permitting up to three mismatches as previously 
described, and mapping the FASTA format sequences to the 
above consensus sequence, allowing up to three mismatches 
as described. The distances between the 5 ′ ends of sense and 
antisense piRNAs were then calculated, and the identities of 
the first and 10th nucleotides from the 5 ′ end of each piRNA 
were recorded. 
DNA methylation analysis 
Adapters and low quality bases were trimmed with Trim Ga- 
lore (parameters: –paired –length 25 –clip_R2 8), and the re- 
sulting clean reads were aligned to the UCSC mm39 genome 
using Bismark (v0.24.2) [ 37 ] with the parameters: –score_min 
L,0,-0.4, which incorporates Bowtie2 (v2.5.4) [ 38 ] for align- 
ment. CpG methylation calls were subsequently extracted 
from the deduplicated mapping output using the Bismark 
methylation extractor. For downstream analyses, only CpGs 
sites covered by at least three reads were retained in each repli- 
cate. 
Methylation levels across genomic features were assessed 
by intersecting CpG positions with genomic annotations us- 
ing bedtools intersect (v2.31.1) [ 39 ] and averaging methyla- 
tion values per feature. Annotations included CpG-islands, 
RefSeq genes, and TEs as mentioned above. Promoters were 
defined as regions spanning −2 kb around RefSeq transcrip- 
tion start sites. Intergenic regions were defined as genomic 
intervals not overlapping any of the aforementioned anno- 
tations. To analyze methylation levels across different repeti- 
tive element classes (repClass) and for individual transposons, 
the aforementioned GTF annotation was processed and inter- 
sected with CpG positions using bedtools intersect (v3.12.7), 
and the results were then visualized in RStudio. The methy- 
lation conversion rate was estimated by mapping all reads to 
the spiked-in CpG unmethylated lambda. 
Assay for transposase-accessible chromatin and 
analysis 
Meiotic spermatocytes and RS isolated via FACS were washed 
with PBS. Nuclei were then prepared by incubating the cells 
in ice-cold lysis buffer [10 mM Tris –HCl (pH 7.4), 10 mM 
NaCl, 3 mM MgCl 2 , 0.1% NP-40] for 10 min, followed by 
centrifugation at 500 g and 4 ◦C for 5 min. Nuclear pellets 
were resuspended in Tn 5 transposase reaction mix for 37 ◦C 
tagmentation (10 min). DNA fragments were then immedi- 
ately purified with A T AC DNA extract beads prior to PCR 
amplification with dual-indexed primers using the TruePrep 
DNA Library Prep Kit V2 for library construction. 
Quality assessment and adaptor sequence removal of raw 
data were carried out using Trim Galore (parameters: –paired 
–length 25 –clip_R2 8), and reads were aligned to the UCSC 
mm39 genome using Bowtie2 (2.5.4) [ 38 ]. Samtools (version 
1.8) was employed for sorting, format conversion, and filter- 
ing of alignment reads. Chromosomal reads from the mito- 
chondria were excluded [ 40 ], and duplicates were removed 
using Sambamba (version 1.0.1) [ 41 ]. Reads aligned to mul- 
tiple locations were discarded, preserving only those uniquely 
aligned to chromosomal locations and properly paired. Peak 
calling was performed using MACS2 (v2.2.7.1) [ 42 ], and 
annotation was carried out using HOMER (5.1) [ 43 ]. To 
identify differential chromatin regions, we used the R pack- 
age DiffBind (3.16.0), and performed an overlap analysis 
with transposon regions to examine the distribution of these 
regions. 
Chromatin immunoprecipitation followed by 
sequencing, and data analysis 
For H3K9me3 chromatin immunoprecipitation followed by 
sequencing(ChIP-seq), the spermatocytes and RS were sorted 
by FACS and were cross-linked with 1% formaldehyde in 
DMEM for 10 min at room temperature. The ChIP experi- 
ments were performed with the ChIP-IT High Sensitivity Kit 
according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Libraries were 
prepared using the VAHTS Universal Pro DNA Library Prep 
Kit for Illumina from Vazyme following the manufacturer’s 
instructions. Libraries were sequenced in 150 bp paired-end 
mode on the DNBSEQ-T7 platform. 
Raw FASTQ files were processed using Trim Galore to re-

–paired –length 25 –stringency 4). Clean paired-end reads 
were then aligned to the mouse reference genome (mm39) 
using Bowtie2 (2.5.4) with default parameters. PCR dupli- 
cates were removed using Sambamba (version 1.0.1), and nor- 
malized genome-wide coverage tracks were generated with 
deepTools (3.5.6) bamCoverage using CPM normalization. 
Genome-wide signal correlations were assessed with multi- 
BigwigSummary, and metaprofiles and heatmaps of young 
LINE1s were generated using computeMatrix and plotHe- 
atmap. Data visualization and statistical analyses were per- 
formed in Rstudio.

Results 
DAXX is a nuclear protein and is highly expressed 
in spermatocytes and early-stage spermatids 
Daxx mRNA is ubiquitously expressed in various organs 
and tissues and is particularly high in the testes of both 
humans and mice ( Supplementary Fig. S1 A, B) [ 44 , 45 ], 
and single-cell sequencing reported relatively higher levels 
in germ cells undergoing meiotic prophase I and in early 
spermatids ( Supplementary Fig. S1 C, D) [ 46 , 47 ]. Although 
Daxx mRNA was detected across various organs and tissues 
( Supplementary Fig. S1 ), DAXX protein was predominantly 
present in the testes of adult mice (Fig. 1 A). Immunoblotting 
of total protein extracts from WT mouse testes at different 
ages [from post-natal day 3 (PD3) to PD35] revealed that 
DAXX protein begins to be expressed at a very low level on 
PD3, notably particularly up to PD14, and peaks on PD21 
(Fig. 1 B). To further explore the biological function of DAXX 
in spermatogenesis, we isolated various developmental stages 
of male germ cells using FACS [ 29 , 30 ]. Western blot analy- 
sis showed that DAXX is highly expressed from the primary 
spermatocytes to the RS stage, but has an extremely low level 
in elongating spermatids and mature sperm (Fig. 1 C). To map 
its intracellular compartmentalization, we performed nuclear 
and cytoplasmic fractionation of mouse testis extracts, and 
western blot analysis revealed that DAXX was present exclu- 
sively in the nucleoplasmic fraction, and it exhibited limited 
association with chromatin, but was almost undetectable in 
cytoplasm (Fig. 1 D). These findings suggest that DAXX could 
be associated with nuclear events in spermatocytes and early- 
stage spermatids. 
To identify the stage-specific role of DAXX, we exam- 
ined its localization and expression in testicular sections from 
adult WT mice. IF analysis revealed that DAXX first appears 
at stages II –IV of the seminiferous epithelial cycle, showing 
prominent accumulation at the XY body in pachytene and 
diplotene spermatocytes. Notably, several intensely stained 
DAXX blobs are observed in the nucleus from mid-pachytene 
to diplotene spermatocytes (Fig. 1 E, arrowheads). While 
DAXX signal persists strongly in diplotene spermatocytes, it 
disappears as cells progress into meiotic metaphase I and reap- 
pear in secondary spermatocytes and RS. No DAXX signal is 
detected in spermatogonia, leptotene/zygotene spermatocytes, 
and condensed spermatids (Fig. 1 E). 
We next determined DAXX’s subcellular localization pat- 
terns via chromosome spread analysis. Precise substaging of 
meiosis was achieved by labeling the synaptonemal complex 
protein SYCP3, a critical component of the axial element. 
Consistent with the above results, we found that DAXX was 
expressed in the nucleus from spermatocytes to RS, with pro- 
nounced enrichment at chromosome ends and the XY body of 
the pachytene and diplotene spermatocytes (Fig. 1 F). To fur- 
ther refine the DAXX localization within the nucleus, we em- 
ployed CREST staining to label the centromeres, and found 
marked aggregation of DAXX at the centromeres (Fig. 1 G), 
suggesting a potential functional association with these struc- 
tures during meiosis. Together, these findings provide strong 
evidence that DAXX is not only present but also may be in- 
volved in critical stages of germ cell development, particularly 
in meiotic chromosome dynamics. This distinct localization 
pattern at centromeres and the XY body highlights DAXX’s 
potential as a key regulatory player in spermatogenesis, un- 
derscoring its importance for maintaining male fertility. 
DAXX deletion leads to impaired spermatogenesis 
and age-dependent fertility loss in male mice 
To investigate the role of DAXX in mammalian spermatogen- 
esis, we developed a conditional knockout mouse model with 
specific deletion of Daxx in germ cells mediated by Ddx4 - 
Cre due to its global loss leading to embryonic lethality [ 26 ]. 
In this model, exons 2 –7 of Daxx are flanked by two loxP 
sites, which are recognized and deleted by Ddx4 -Cre recom- 
binase in testis ( Daxx flox/ −, Ddx4 -Cre mice, referred to as 
Daxx cKO ) ( Supplementary Fig. S2 A). The Ddx4 -Cre trans- 
gene exhibits germ cell-restricted activation from embryonic 
day 15 (E15) and continues post-natally [ 48 ]. PCR-based 
genotyping analyses of mouse tail confirmed the genotypes 
of Daxx cKO , Daxx flox/ + , and Daxx + / + mice ( Supplementary 
Fig. S2 B). Western blot assay indicated that the DAXX pro- 
tein level was significantly decreased in Daxx cKO testes com- 
pared with the control testes ( Supplementary Fig. S2 C). IF 
analysis also demonstrated depletion of DAXX protein within 
Ddx4 -positive germ cells in Daxx cKO testes, while standard 
expression patterns were maintained in control littermates 
( Supplementary Fig. S2 D).These findings collectively indi- 
cate that DAXX was successfully knocked out in male germ 
cells. 
Subsequently, fertility tests revealed that this was almost 
normal for Daxx cKO males up to 4 months old as they pro- 
duced comparable numbers of offspring as the controls (Fig. 
2 A). However, WT females mated with Daxx cKO males at 
mid-adulthood (5 –6 months of age) and later produced much 
smaller litter sizes (Fig. 2 A). The testes from 3-, 6-, and 14- 
month-old Daxx cKO mice were significantly smaller than those 
of the controls (Fig. 2 B, C). We speculate that DAXX loss may 
cause an age-dependent decline in male fertility, so we per- 
formed histological analysis of testes and epididymis in dif- 
ferent age groups. In contrast to the seminiferous epithelium 
of control mice, which had the typical arrangement of sper- 
matogenic cells including spermatogonia, spermatocytes, RS, 
and elongating spermatids from the basal membrane to the lu- 
men, Daxx cKO mice exhibited an age-dependent loss of germ 
cells and extensive defects across the 12 stages of the semi- 
niferous epithelium (Fig. 2 D). We also quantified the sperm 
in the cauda epididymis and found that Daxx cKO mice had 
significantly fewer sperm than control mice (Fig. 2 E). The 
testes from 6-month-old Daxx cKO male mice displayed pro- 
gressive germ cell depletion during spermatogenic stages, initi- 
ating from early germ cells (spermatogonia) to late germ cells 
(elongated spermatids) (Fig. 2 F). An age-dependent increase 
of the percentage of abnormal tubules was observed, while 
the population of spermatogonia progressively decreases in 
Daxx cKO male mice ( Supplementary Fig. S2 E –H). Further-

Given that extensive apoptosis in spermatocytes and signifi- 
cant reduction in post-meiotic cells were observed in Daxx cKO 
testis, we first hypothesized that DAXX loss could result 
in meiotic defects. To explore this, we examined synapsis

or γH2AX signal between Ctrl and Daxx cKO spermatocytes 
( Supplementary Fig. S4 A, B), suggesting that there were no 
defects in synapsis and DSB repair in spermatocytes after

Loss of Daxx causes longer manchette 
microtubules and damaged acrosomes during 
spermiogenesis 
To further investigate the spermiogenic defects in Daxx cKO 
mice based on the above observation of increased apoptotic 
spermatids, we assessed the sperm parameters and found that 
the proportion of mature sperm with head malformations 
in the epididymis of Daxx cKO mice was higher than that 
in control mice (Fig. 3 A, B). Additionally, computer-assisted 
sperm analysis (C AS A) revealed a reduction in sperm motil- 
ity in Daxx cKO mice (Fig. 3 C). Sperm head malformations 
frequently correlate with damaged acrosomal formation and 
manchette structure [ 49 ]. These structures play pivotal roles 
in the elongation of spermatids through their functional syn- 
ergy [ 50 , 51 ]. The acrosome is a special kind of organelle 
with a cap-like structure that covers the head of the sper- 
matid [ 52 , 53 ]. The manchette is a typical non-centrosomal 
microtubule (MT)-enriched structure [ 54 ]. To analyze the 
manchette MTs comprehensively, we employed transmission 
electron microscopy (TEM) imaging of testis sections, along 
with immunostaining (Fig. 3 D –F). The results showed that 
while Daxx cKO spermatids maintained structurally normal 
manchette MTs up to step 7, the manchette MTs surrounding 
the nucleus became abnormally elongated during steps 8 –10 
of spermatid development (Fig. 3 D). Additionally, abnormally 
longer manchette MTs were also shown in frozen testis sec- 
tions of Daxx cKO mice (Fig. 3 E). To eliminate the potential 
observation bias from the different direction of the section, we 
performed IF staining of single spermatids (Fig. 3 F). Consis- 
tent with the above findings, the MTs of the manchette in elon- 
gating spermatids from Daxx cKO mice continued to lengthen, 
resulting in a longer and thinner nucleus that lacked the char- 
acteristic hook shape as spermiogenesis progressed when com- 
pared with those from the controls (Fig. 3 F, G). PNA signals 
also exhibited a non-uniform distribution in elongating sper- 
matids in Daxx cKO mice (Fig. 3 F). To determine the acroso- 
mal damage, ultrastructural profiling of spermatozoa was per- 
formed through TEM. In contrast to spermatozoa from con- 
trol mice, which exhibited long oval shapes with their acro- 
somes attached to the nuclei, the acrosome of Daxx cKO males 
showed dissociation from the nuclear envelope, presenting as 
a shedding and pleating appearance but normal “9 + 2”struc- 
ture (Fig. 3 H). These findings suggest that DAXX is essen- 
tial for the acrosome formation and the organization of the 
manchette MT network during spermiogenesis. 
Given DAXX’s role as a molecular chaperone for his- 
tone H3.3, it is likely that it participates in the histone-to- 
protamine replacement during sperm maturation. To inves- 
tigate this, we assessed nuclear condensation by staining for 
transition protein 1 (TNP1) and protamine 1 (PRM1), and ob- 
served that a marked reduction in the fluorescence intensity of 
TNP1 and PRM1 in the testicular sections of Daxx cKO mice 
when compared with the controls ( Supplementary Fig. S5 A, 
B). Additionally, western blot analysis revealed a significant 
decrease of PRM1 protein expression in the Daxx cKO testes 
( Supplementary Fig. S5 C). To eliminate the possibility that 
the reduced PRM1 levels resulted from a reduction in sperm 
count (as indicated in Fig. 2 E), we collected mature sperm 
from the cauda epididymis and performed western blot to 
evaluate PRM1’s abundance. The results still reported a signif- 
icant reduction in PRM1 levels of mature sperm in Daxx cKO 
mice ( Supplementary Fig. S5 D). We also quantified the lev- 
els of histone H3 in the testicular section and found that it 
has been replaced by PRM1 in tubules from stages XII to 
I ( Supplementary Fig. S5 E). These findings suggest thart al- 
though there was a reduction in PRM1 abundance due to 
sperm defects, the process of histone-to-protamine replace- 
ment remained unaffected.

DAXX loss alters the transcriptome of germ cells 
The absence of DAXX may disrupt the expression of spe- 
cific genes critical for spermatogenesis, leading to severe apop- 
tosis of germ cells and abnormal sperm morphology. We 
next performed genome-wide transcriptome analyses of iso- 
lated spermatocytes and RS, and identified 60 significantly 
up-regulated genes and 22 significantly down-regulated genes 
in Daxx -deficient meiotic cells ( Supplementary Fig. S7 A –C; 
Supplementary Table S2 ). In contrast, DAXX-deficient RS 
exhibited much greater changes in gene expression; 2144 
genes were up-regulated and 176 genes were down-regulated 
( Supplementary Fig. S7 A –C; Supplementary Table S2 ). The 
down-regulated genes in both meiotic and post-meiotic cells 
were enriched in cell membrane components ( Supplementary 
Fig. S7 D, E), while many up-regulated genes were associated 
with RNA polymerase II-mediated transcription and apop- 
totic pathways ( Supplementary Fig. S7 D, E). Notably, genes 
associated with actin cytoskeleton organization were down- 
regulated in DAXX-deficient RS ( Supplementary Fig. S7 E), 
which may be one of the reasons for the aforementioned 
manchette MT abnormalities observed in Daxx cKO mice. A 
comparative analysis of meiotic and post-meiotic transcrip- 
tomes in control cells confirmed previous findings [ 57 , 58 ] that 
the transition from meiosis to post-meiosis involves extensive 
transcriptional reprogramming, in which thousands of genes 
either up- or down-regulated were organized into distinct clus- 
ters ( Supplementary Fig. S7 F, G). DAXX deletion resulted in 
notable shifts in gene regulation during the transition. Specifi- 
cally, a limited subset of genes were down-regulated, whereas a 
large number were up-regulated in Daxx cKO cells, particularly 
those involved in transcription. However, the overall cluster- 
ing patterns remained unaffected ( Supplementary Fig. S7 G). 
Although gene reprogramming followed similar trends in both 
control and Daxx cKO cells, the number of up-regulated genes 
was greater in Daxx cKO cells ( Supplementary Fig. S7 F). Taken 
together, these findings highlight DAXX’s essential role in reg- 
ulating the transcriptional landscape of germ cells and it has 
different effects on specific genes of different developmental 
processes during spermatogenesis. 
Transposons are derepressed in Daxx cKO mice 
Besides transcriptional repression, DAXX also plays a critical 
role in silencing ERVs in murine embryonic stem cells [ 59 –63 ]. 
Other studies have also demonstrated that the loss of Daxx 
in the mouse pancreas has been linked to the derepression 
of ERVs, leading to increased expression of nearby protein- 
coding genes [ 64 ]. 
To investigate the effects of DAXX deletion on TE ex- 
pression, we conducted transcriptomic analysis and found 
significant up-regulation of two retrotransposon subsets, i.e. 
27 differentially expressed ERVs (20 ERVK, 3 ERVL, and 4 
ERV1) and 12 differentially expressed LINE1s in Daxx cKO 
meiotic cells. A different pattern emerged in Daxx cKO post- 
meiotic cells; 34 differentially expressed ERVs (25 ERVK, 4 
ER VL, and 5 ER V1) and 8 differentially expressed LINE1s 
showed a significant increase with a P -value < 0.05 and 
log 2 fold change (FC) > 0.5 (Fig. 4 A). Evolutionarily young 
LINE1s are capable of autonomous transposition and their 
activation compromises genomic stability. In Daxx cKO mei- 
otic cells, young LINE1s, including L1Md_A, L1Md_Gf, and 
L1Md_Tf, were up-regulated; however, they were not up- 
regulated in Daxx cKO RS (Fig. 4 A, B). We further examined 
the expression of LINE1 protein in Daxx cKO testes and found 
that LINE1 ORF1P protein was barely detectable in control 
tubules, as expected. In contrast, Daxx cKO testes exhibited a 
much higher abundance of LINE1 ORF1P in spermatocytes 
(Fig. 4 C, D). Correspondingly, western blot analysis reported 
an elevated LINE1 ORF1P protein in Daxx cKO testes (Fig. 4 E, 
F). Overactivated LINE1 ORF1P was observed in germ cells 
of Daxx cKO testes as early as on PD14 when late zygotene or 
early pachytene cells appeared during the first wave of sper- 
matogenesis (Fig. 4 G). DAXX deletion also led to overexpres- 
sion of one specific ERV subfamily IAPEy in Daxx cKO meiotic 
cells (which phenocopied the Miwi2 - [ 20 ] and Spocd1 - [ 19 ] 
deficient spermatocytes) as well as Daxx cKO post-meiotic cells 
(Fig. 4 A). MMERVK10c was also significantly up-regulated in 
Daxx cKO RS (Fig. 4 A). In

conclusion, DAXX deficiency results 
in derepression of a large number of TE families, particularly 
the young LINE1s in meiotic cells. 
DAXX participates in LINE1 repression by 
maintaining LINE1 DNA methylation but not the 
piRNA pathway 
The overactivation of retrotransposons in Daxx cKO mice 
closely resembles the phenotype observed in piRNA pathway- 
deficient mutants [ 65 –67 ]. It is well documented that 
pachytene-stage piRNAs derive from a defined set of 100 TE- 
poor genomic regions defined as piRNA clusters [ 35 ] and 
their function is to silence active transposons by regulating the 
translation of their target transcripts in spermatocytes [ 68 ]. 
Therefore, we next asked whether DAXX functions in repress- 
ing transposons via the piRNA pathway. We examined the 
pachytene piRNA-related proteins MIWI (PIWIL1) and MILI 
(PIWIL2) by western blot and IF, and found that both of them 
exhibited similar expression in Daxx cKO testes to that in the 
controls ( Supplementary Fig. S8 ).

deletion, we performed small RNA sequencing on meiotic 
cells and RS from control and Daxx cKO testes, and found that

spermatogenesis. 
DNA methylation serves as the principal epigenetic mod- 
ification responsible for repression of TEs. To interrogate 
DAXX’s regulatory influence on TE DNA methylation, whole- 
genome bisulfite sequencing (WGBS) was implemented on iso- 
lated meiotic cells and RS from control and Daxx cKO testes. 
Indeed, we observed no significant change in global methyla- 
tion in Daxx cKO RS, but a statistically significant yet modest 
decrease in methylation was observed in genome genic, intra- 
genic, promoter, and transposon regions of Daxx cKO meiotic 
cells (Fig. 5 C, D). DNA methylation patterns were also as- 
sessed in individual TE families. Globally, we observed a sig- 
nificant hypomethylation in LINE, ER VK, ER VL, ER V1, and 
SINE of Daxx cKO meiotic cells, but there was no significant 
change in Daxx cKO RS (Fig. 5 E, F). Consistently, young LINE1 
subfamilies L1Md_A, L1Md_Tf, and L1Md_Gf displayed a 
significant demethylation in Daxx cKO meiotic cells but not in 
Daxx cKO RS (Fig. 5 G). 
Notably, LINE1s are also up-regulated in spermatocytes of 
Daxx flox/ −, Stra8 -Cre mice (Fig. 5 H, I), demonstrating that 
their activation is not attributable to defects in de novo DNA 
methylation. The observed age-dependent loss of spermato- 
gonia in Daxx -deficient models highlights a likely require- 
ment for DAXX in spermatogonial proliferation, a hypoth- 
esis awaiting rigorous experimental validation. We previously 
hypothesized that the LINE1 activation and hypomethylation 
in meiotic cells of Daxx cKO mice might arise from defects in 
de novo methylation in PGCs rather than impaired mainte- 
nance of methylation during meiosis. To test this, we ana- 
lyzed LINE1 expression in Stra8 -Cre-mediated DAXX dele- 
tion testis ( Stra8 expression initiates at PD5 in male mice, 
whereas genome-wide de novo methylation is largely com- 
plete at PD2.5 [ 18 ]). Consistently, LINE1 was not activated 
and DNA methylation was not reduced in PD8 Daxx cKO testes 
(Supplementary Fig. S11 ), when de novo methylation had 
been completed. These results indicate that LINE1 activation 
is caused by maintenance defects of LINE1 DNA methylation 
in Daxx cKO meiotic cells. 
We employed a proteomics approach and performed anti- 
DAXX immunoprecipitation coupled with quantitative MS 
(IP-MS/MS) from testicular protein extracts ( Supplementary 
Fig. S12 A; Supplementary Table S6 ) and identified DAXX- 
interacting proteins that are potentially involved in the under- 
lying mechanisms, including DMAP1 and MAP1B. Biochem- 
ical assays demonstrate that DMAP1 (DNMT1-associated 
protein 1) strongly enhances the methyltransferase activity of 
DNMT1, implying its role as a cofactor in the maintenance of 
DNA methylation during replication [ 69 ]. In vitro biochem- 
ical characterization revealed a direct interaction between 
DMAP1 and DAXX [ 70 ], suggesting potential functional col- 
laboration. We confirmed the co-precipitation of DAXX with 
DMAP1 in vivo . DMAP1 was identified in DAXX-IP com- 
plexes from testis lysates, and their association was confirmed 
by reciprocal IP experiments (Fig. 5 J). In addition, western 
blot analysis of the DMAP1 protein was decreased in Daxx cKO 
testes (Fig. 5 K). 
MAP1B was identified as the second significantly enriched 
protein. MAP1B belongs to the MT-associated protein fam- 
ily, which is believed to be involved in microtubule assem- 
bly [ 71 ]. We verified this interaction by Co-IP experiments 
( Supplementary Fig. S12 B). Furthermore, the manchette MT 
minus end localization of MAP1B was reduced in Daxx cKO 
mice ( Supplementary Fig. S12 C). 
Loss of Daxx results in decreased H3K9me3 
enrichment at young LINE1s 
ChIP-seq analysis of H3K9me3 revealed that the genome- 
wide distribution of H3K9me3 was not altered in Daxx cKO 
spermatocytes. However, the H3K9me3 level at the sex body 
(consisting of the X and Y chromosomes) was markedly re- 
duced, whereas no such change was detected in RS (Fig. 6 A). 
This selective reduction may be related to the high enrich- 
ment of DAXX on the sex chromosomes (Fig. 1 ). Although 
most LINE1s are located on autosomes in terms of total copy 
number, previous studies indicate that the sex chromosomes, 
particularly the X chromosome, display a higher LINE1 den- 
sity, defined as a greater proportion of LINE1s per unit chro- 
mosomal length, than autosomes in human [ 72 ]. This dis- 
tribution pattern is observed in mice, and L1Md_A on the 
sex chromosomes accounts for nearly a quarter of the to- 
tal population (Fig. 6 B). Notably, we observed a marked re- 
duction of H3K9me3 at young LINE1s on the XY chromo- 
somes (Fig. 6 C; Supplementary Fig. S13 A), whereas LINE1- 
associated H3K9me3 levels in RS showed no obvious changes 
( Supplementary Fig. S13 B). 
To delineate Daxx -dependent chromatin-remodeling ef- 
fects, A T AC-seq of meiotic spermatocytes and RS revealed 
accessible chromatin domains, with no significant differences 
in peak distribution across genomic regions between the two 
genotypes of males (Fig. 6 E). Comparative chromatin accessi- 
bility analysis between control and Daxx cKO meiotic cells re- 
vealed 4473 differentially accessible regions, comprising 330 
open regions and 4143 closed regions (the original data are 
available in Supplementay Table S6). We next evaluated all 
differentially accessible regions and found that 50.91% (168 
of 330) of the differentially open regions contained TE se- 
quences and 15.21% (630 of 4143) of the differentially closed 
regions contained TE sequences. Notably, young LINE1s were 
preferentially localized to differentially open regions (21 re- 
gions) over differentially closed regions (9 regions) (Fig. 6 F). In 
RS, the proportion of differentially open TEs was lower than 
that of closed TEs. The above findings suggest that DAXX 
deletion may activate the expression of young LINE1s by al- 
tering chromatin accessibility in spermatocytes. 
Taken together, these data suggest that Daxx cKO spermato- 
cytes exhibit reduced H3K9me3 enrichment at LINE1s, con- 
comitant with increased chromatin accessibility.

Discussion 
DAXX serves as an essential protein with a multifaceted in- 
teractome, whose disruption results in embryonic lethality 
at E6.5 accompanied by extensive apoptosis, demonstrating 
DAXX governs LINE1 silencing during spermatogenesis

its indispensable role in embryonic development [ 26 ]. In the 
current work, we have carried out an in-depth study on the 
role of DAXX in mouse spermatogenesis. Germ cell-specific 
Daxx deletion models coupled with multi-dimensional profil- 
ing have allowed us to demonstrate DAXX as a vital factor in 
mammalian spermatogenesis. 
The Daxx cKO male mice exhibited germ cell loss and an 
age-dependent decline in fertility. The morphology of the sper- 
matozoa was highly altered and the sperm concentration was 
strongly decreased in Daxx cKO mice. In particular, excessive 
elongation of the manchette MTs of spermatids occurs in 
Daxx cKO mice. The manchette assembles by MT –actin to form 
a dynamic cytoskeleton, which creates a transient intracellu- 
lar structure during the phase of sperm head shaping [ 73 –75 ]. 
Manchette assembly initiates at step 8 of spermiogenesis , tem- 
porally coupled with nuclear elongation, and undergoes disas- 
sembly during steps 13 –14 concurrent with nuclear architec- 
tural finalization. Aberrant manchette structure impairs nu- 
clear shaping, resulting in defective sperm head morphogen- 
esis [ 54 , 74 ]. We found that DAXX plays a role in the orga- 
nization of the manchette MT network and a single cluster 
consisting of significantly down-regulated actin cytoskeleton 
organization in Daxx cKO RS. However, the in-depth reasons 
for the defect deserve continued investigation. 
Numerous articles have reported the association of DAXX 
with ERVs, but few have reported the correlation between 
DAXX and young LINE1s. Here we have analyzed the expres- 
sion of the repetitive elements during spermatogenesis. Deple- 
tion of DAXX triggered mass retrotransposon transcriptional 
activation in both meiotic cells and RS; therein the dysregu- 
lation of MMERVK10C-int [ 9 ] and L1Md_A [ 5 ] retrotrans- 
posons are likely to be implicated in infertility. Young LINE1 
families exemplified by L1Md_A, L1Md_Tf, and L1Md_Gf 
were up-regulated in meiotic cells; however, they were not 
up-regulated in Daxx cKO post-meiotic cells. Considering that 
DAXX deficiency does not significantly alter DNA methyla- 
tion patterns at young LINE1s in post-meiotic cells, we hy- 
pothesized that some meiotic cells escaped transposon dere- 
pression in Daxx cKO spermatocytes and passed through meio- 
sis into RS. These findings demonstrate that DAXX’s tran- 
scriptional repression of young LINE1s during meiosis is not 
globally required. 
Daxx deletion triggers LINE1 activation and apoptosis 
in germ cells, and a subsequent decrease in post-meiotic 
cell populations, yet conventional cytological assessment fails 
to detect obvious disruption of chromosomal behavior dur- 
ing meiosis, suggesting that defects probably arise after the 
diplotene stage in the progression to RS or subtler functional 
impairments. 
Our study established that DAXX interacts with DMAP1 
in mouse testes, and specifically demonstrates that DAXX 
knockout significantly reduces DMAP1 protein levels in testic- 
ular lysates. This observation validates the proposal put for- 
ward by Ryuta Muromoto et al. [ 70 ], wherein DAXX pro- 
tects DMAP1 from degradation in vivo . DNMT1, an essen- 
tial enzyme for DNA methylation maintenance, exhibits ∼2.5- 
fold elevated methyltransferase activity upon DMAP1 bind- 
ing in vitro . Therefore, we proposed that DAXX may stabilize 
DMAP1, thereby facilitating DMAP1-dependent potentiation 
of DNMT1 activity during meiosis. This pathway could con- 
tribute to the maintenance of DNA methylation and, conse- 
quently, to the suppression of young LINE1s. The limitation 
lies in the unresolved chromatin localization of DAXX and 
DMAP1, as well as the lack of functional rescue experiments 
assessing whether DMAP1 overexpression would ameliorate 
the defects caused by Daxx loss. While IF staining has delin- 
eated DAXX’s subcellular distribution at a coarse resolution, 
the lack of an effective antibody for IP precluded precise map- 
ping. To address this, future work will be needed to employ 
mouse models coupled with ChIP-seq to systematically inves- 
tigate the correlation of DAXX/DMAP1 binding and DNA 
hypomethylation on LINE1s. 
Dnmt3A KO and Prdm1 - Dnmt3A cKO mutants exhib- 
ited a progressive age-dependent decline in spermatogene- 
sis, such that only spermatogenesis-free tubules remained at 
6 months [ 16 ]—a phenotype shared with Daxx cKO mice, 
prompting future studies to focus on the role of DAXX in reg- 
ulating spermatogonial stem cells. However, DNMT3A does 
not silence retrotransposons during spermatogenesis, which 
distinguishes it from DAXX. DNMT3C primarily functions 
in de novo DNA methylation during embryonic development, 
whereas we propose that DAXX plays a role in the mainte- 
nance of DNA methylation during meiosis. In Dnmt3C KO 
mice, differentially methylated regions (DMRs) are predom- 
inantly enriched at the 5 ′ -untranslated region (5 ′ UTR) of 
LINE1s [ 16 ]. In contrast, DMRs are distributed from the 5 ′ 
UTR to the 3 ′ UTR in Daxx cKO mice, and the reduction is 
less severe than that observed in Dnmt3C K O . This differ - 
ence may be attributed to a role for DAXX in maintaining 
methylation, which could also explain why LINE1 activa- 
tion is less pronounced in Daxx cKO mice than in the Dnmt3C 
mutant. 
Considering the specific localization of DAXX in the cen- 
tromere regions of the spermatocytes, we speculate that 
DAXX is likely to be involved in the first meiotic division as it 
has been demonstrated that ATRX recruits DAXX to pericen- 
tric heterochromatin in pre-ovulatory oocytes [ 76 ], and loss 
of maternal ATRX function at the metaphase II stage disrupts 
chromosome alignment and adversely affects female fertility 
[ 76 –78 ]. Interestingly, oocytes from Daxx cKO females retain 
the ability to support pre-implantation embryonic develop- 
ment ( Supplementary Fig. S14 A –F). In the same way, DAXX 
deficiency does not affect chromosome arrangement in mei- 
otic metaphase I cells of male mice ( Supplementary Fig. S14 G). 
This reveals that DAXX and ATRX make distinct functional 
contributions to oocyte development.

The mRNA-seq, small RNA-seq, WGBS, A T AC-seq, and 
ChIP-seq data are available in the Gene Expression Om- 
nibus under the accession numbers GSE293055, GSE315860, 
GSE315864, and GSE315982. The mass spectrometry pro- 
teomics data have been deposited to the ProteomeXchange 
Consortium ( https://proteomecentral.proteomexchange.org ) 
via the iProX partner repository [ 79 , 80 ] with the dataset 
identifier PXD072798. This study did not generate any cus- 
tom code. Any additional information required to reanalyze 
the data reported herein is available from the corresponding 
author on request. .
